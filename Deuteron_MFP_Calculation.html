Deuteron Mean Free Path Calculation

Most of the math used in this calculation can be found either from my meeting with Chris or from the Davis and Petrov article. The first step to calculating the MFP will be to find the stopping power. From the Davis and Petrov article, I know that $$ S_{D-X}(E) = S_{H-X}(\frac{E}{2}) $$ Here X will need to be O and D, because we are dealing with a D₂O vapor. Since the PSTAR calculates proton stopping power, which is basically a Hydrogen atom. I will use this in a material of both Hydrogen (to estimate the \( S_{D-D} \) stopping power), and Oxygen. If we want deuterons of 2.5MeV, then we want to find the PSTAR values for Hydrogen at 1.25MeV based on the above equation. For a proton with incident energy of 1.25MeV (from the Hah and Petrov article) and Hydrogen, PSTAR says that there is a total stopping power of \( 5.673 \times 10^{2} \frac{MeV cm^{2}}{g} \) and a CDSA of \( 1.253 \times 10^{-3} \frac{g}{cm^{2}} \). For the PSTAR with Oxygen at 1.25MeV, we have the \( 1.858 \times 10^{2} \frac{MeV cm^{2}}{g} \) and a CDSA of \( 4.265 \times 10^{-3} \frac{g}{cm^{2}} \).

Using the second equation from the Davis and Petrov article, we can use $$S_{D-D_{2}O}(E) = S_{D-O}(E)n_{O} + S_{D-D}(E)n_{D}$$ Now I need to find the number density of deuterium and oxygen in the chamber, which I can find by assuming that the pressure in the chamber is made up of non-interacting monatomic D₂O vapor. $$ n_{D_{2}O} = n_{O} \mbox{ and } n_{D_{2}O} = \frac{1}{2}n_{D} $$ I will treat this as classical ideal gas even though we are working with a pressure of ~1 Torr. Using the ideal gas equation, we have $$ PV=Nk_{B}T $$ For the number density we can take \( \frac{N}{V} \). Taking this from the ideal gas equation we will have $$ \frac{N}{V} = \frac{P}{k_{B}T} $$ I am going to assume that T remains close to room temperature, I will approximate T~300K. This will give a close enough approximation. 1 Torr of pressure is around 133.3 Pa. Combining all of this we get: $$ n_{D_{2}O} = \frac{P}{k_{B}T} = \frac{133.3 Pa} {1.38 \times 10^{-23} \frac{J}{K} * 300K} = 3.22 \times 10^{22} \frac{\mbox{particles}}{m^{3}} = 3.22 \times 10^{16} \frac{\mbox{particles}}{cm^{3}} $$ In order to use the stopping power I found from NIST, I will need the mass density of the particles instead of the number density. I believe that the Davis paper had a stopping power different than ours, so number density worked for them. The number density of the oxygen is \( n_{O} = n_{D_{2}O} = 3.22 \times 10^{16} \frac{\mbox{particles}}{cm^{3} \) and the number density of deuterium is \( n_{D} = 2n_{D_{2}O} = 6.44 \times 10^{16} \) Converting these to mass density: $$ \rho = nm \mbox{ (for 1 particle in grams)} $$ The mass of a each particle is 16 AMU or \( 2.66 \times 10^{-23} \)g for O and 2 AMU or \(3.32 \times 10^{-24} \)g for each D. The mass densities are $$ \rho_{O} = 3.22 \times 10^{16} \frac{\mbox{particles}}{cm^{3} 2.66 \times 10^{-23} \frac{g}{\mbox{particle}} = 8.57 \times 10^{-7} \frac{g}{cm^{3}} $$ and $$ \rho_{D} = 6.44 \times 10^{16} \frac{\mbox{particles}}{cm^{3} 3.32 \times 10^{-24} \frac{g}{\mbox{particle}} = 2.14 \times 10^{-7} \frac{g}{cm^{3}} $$
