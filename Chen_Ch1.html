Chapter 1: Introduction

Section 1

Our universe is mostly made of plasma. However, we do not see this because of the Saha equation, which gives the amount of ionization to be expected in a gas in thermal equilibrium: $$ \frac{n_{i}}{n_{n}} \approx 2.4 \times 10^{21} \frac{T^{3/2}}{n_{i}} e^{-U_{i}/k_{B}T} $$ Where the variables are:

  • \(n_{i}\) and \(n_{n}\) are the number density in m³ of the ionized and neutral atoms
  • T is the temperature of the gas
  • \(k_{B}\) is the Boltzmann constant
  • \(U_{i}\) is the ionization energy of the gas (the energy to remove one electron from the outer shell)
The \(n_{i}\) on the RHS of the equation means that the ratio of ionized atoms to neutral atoms depends on the density of ionized atoms. This is becuase the recombination rate of ionized atoms depends on how many ionized particles there are to recombine with. The average ratio of ionized to neutral particles at room temperture is ~\(10^{-122}\).

Section 2

Plasma: A quasineutral gas of charged and neutral particles which exhibits collective behavior.

  • Collective Behavior: Motions that depend not only on local conditions but on the state of the plasma in remote regions as well. This means that our ions and electrons are effected by the Coulomb force, which has a behavior of 1/r². This is the local behavior since it falls as r increases. However, if we take a solid angle of a plasma and increase r, the volume of plasma that will effect our particle will grow with r³. This means that as we increase r, more plasma will effect the motion of our particle, leading to the effect of the remote behavior in our collective behavior.
  • Quasineutral: If the dimensions L of a system are much larger than \(\lambda_{D}\), then whenever lcoal concentrations of charge arise or external potentials are introduced into the system, these are shielded out in a distance short compared with L. This leaves the bulk of the plasma free of large electric potentials and fields.
