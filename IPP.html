Session 1

We will be using eV as an Energy/Temperature unit (11,400K \( \approx \) 1eV).
Plasmas start around 13.6 eV (Hydrogen ionizaiton) or \(10^5\)K. This course will focus on the regime of 1eV-100keV.


Definitions:

  • Plasma: A quasineutral gas of charged (and neutral) particles in which the particle ineractions are predominantly collective.
  • Quasineutral: Approximatly equal numbers of positively and negatively charged particles on a scale that is long compared to the collective interaction scale length.
  • Collective Interactions: Charged particles interact simulteneously with many other charged particles (not just 2-body interactions).

Questions

  1. How does one describe such a collective interaction medium?
  2. Do charged particles really have infinite interaction distances?
    • No, the interactions are limited by polarizing effects of charged particles (see Debye Length soon)

Calculate \( \phi \) around a "Test" particle

We will make several assumptions. First we will assume electrostatics so we get the following equations: \[\vec{E} = -\vec{\nabla} \phi\] \[\vec{\nabla} \cdot \vec{E} = \frac{\rho}{\epsilon_{o}} = - \nabla^2 \phi\]

We need to find \(\rho\) to find \(\phi\):

  1. Test charge is given as \(-q_{T} \delta (\vec{x} - \vec{x}_{T}) \)
  2. Polarization charge \(\delta \rho_{P}\) due to \(\phi\) in the plasma

If we assume a Gibb's distribution of particles (f \( \sim e^{\frac{-H}{kT}}\)) \[\eta \simeq \eta_{o} e^{\frac{-q\phi}{T}} \] If we expand around small \(\frac{-q\phi}{T}\), then we get: \[\eta \simeq \eta_{o} [1-\frac{q\phi}{T} + ...] \] The density \(\delta \rho_{P}\) can be expressed as a sum of the particles: \[\delta \rho_{P} = \sum_{j} \eta_{j} q_{j} = \sum_{j} q_{j}\eta_{o}[1-\frac{q\phi}{T} + ...]\] This gives us a mathematical definition of quasineutrality \(\rightarrow \sum_{j} \eta_{j} q_{j} = 0 \)
Continuing on, this yeilds: \[\delta \rho_{P} = -\sum_{j} \frac{\eta_{oj} q_{j}^2}{T_{J}}\phi\] Solving this gives: \[-\nabla^2 \phi = \frac{\rho}{\epsilon_{o}}\] \[= \frac{q_{T}}{\epsilon_{o}} \delta (\vec{x} - \vec{x_{o}}) - \sum_{j} \frac{\eta_{oj} q_{j}^2}{T_{J} \epsilon_{o}}\phi\] Let us define the Debye Length as \( \frac{1}{\lambda_{D}^2} = \frac{\eta_{oj} q_{j}^2}{T_{J} \epsilon_{o}} \), which leaves us with: \[ \nabla^2 - \frac{1}{\lambda_{D}^2} \phi = -\frac{q_{T}}{\epsilon_{o}} \delta (\vec{x} - \vec{x_{o}}) \] The \(\frac{1}{\lambda_{D}^2}\) is what contributes to the Debye sheilding. The Debye sheilding kills the electric potential with respect to r. The formal solution to this is given by: \[\phi(\vec{x}) = \frac{q_{T} e^\frac{-|\vec{x}-\vec{x_{o}}|}{\lambda_{D}}{4 \pi \epsilon_{o} |\vec{x}-\vec{x_{o}}|}\]
